BACKGROUND

What is DNA Design?

The Data and Analytics (DNA) Design team supports SAP Analytics Cloud (SAC), SAP Datasphere (DSP), and SAP Business Data Cloud (BDC), products that help businesses unify, visualize, and analyze data at scale. The team spans 8 countries with 165+ designers, researchers, and technical writers.


THE PROBLEM

165+ designers. No shared design library.

SAP's Web UI Kit covers the basics, but not the DNA-specific patterns and components our products actually need. Three challenges emerged:

CHALLENGE #1: INCREASED DESIGN EFFORT

Building custom components from scratch increases design time and reduces efficiency.

CHALLENGE #2: INCONSISTENT IMPLEMENTATION

When designers create their own versions of the same components, UI inconsistencies emerge across DNA products.

CHALLENGE #3: POOR SCALABILITY & MAINTENANCE

Adapting designs for new features becomes inefficient without a shared, maintainable foundation.

THE SOLUTION

Introducing the DNA Web UI Kit

The DNA Web UI Kit builds on SAP's existing design system to centralize custom components, patterns, page layouts, and guidelines. Each component page includes usage guidelines, common usage examples, visual specs for developers, and accessibility annotations.

COMPONENT SPOTLIGHT: DATA TOKEN

A deep dive into one of the kit's most complex components.

The Data Token required aligning up to 10 contributors across multiple products before a single frame was built. Here's how it came together.

Step 1: Stakeholder Alignment

The Data Token existed in fragmented forms across SAC and DSP. Each product had developed its own variation independently. I gathered all existing instances, coordinated with 10 designers across both products, and facilitated alignment on a single unified proposal before any new design work began.

After: A consolidated proposal for SAC and DSP



Result: An aligned design across products


Step 2: Building the Component

Designing for flexibility, theming, and accessibility.

With the proposal approved by design leads, I built the Data Token in Figma, accounting for multiple variants, theming support, minimum touch targets, and keyboard navigation. I also documented usage guidelines and curated real-world examples to make adoption as straightforward as possible.


Step 3: Collaborating with Development

Learning to speak the same language.

Working across 4 development teams, I quickly realized I needed to actually understand how they were building. I dug into their CSS files, explored the UI5 Demo Kit, and got familiar with the variables and component names they were already using, so my annotations made sense in their context, not just mine.

I also stopped waiting until implementation to get their input. Getting a developer's eyes on specs early caught a lot of issues before they became actual problems. After implementation, I reviewed the live test server using browser inspection tools, documented inconsistencies, and gave final UX sign-off after revisions.

ROLLOUT STRATEGY

Official Launch

Introducing the Web Kit to the organization.

I presented the DNA Web UI Kit at the global Q2 DNA Design meeting, demoing components to teams across time zones, aligning on shared processes, and opening it up for questions and feedback. Within three weeks, the Data Token alone had been implemented over 1,000 times.

Ongoing Strategy Development

How do we drive adoption and streamline communications?

The kit launched with momentum, but no infrastructure to sustain it. There was no central place for designers to find updates, no defined release cadence, and no clear feedback channel. Past communications had been ad hoc, making it hard for the broader DNA Design team to track what was available or what was coming next.

Developing a Plan

Building the infrastructure for long-term adoption.

When I returned to the team full-time after my internship, I partnered with a senior design systems designer to define our strategy across three areas: a central resource hub, a structured release cadence, and dedicated communication channels.

The strategy was presented to the Head of Design and the DNA leadership team, then rolled out to the broader design team starting Q4 2025.


What's new?

DNA DESIGN SYSTEMS SHAREPOINT

A central portal for release notes, video tutorials, and FAQs.

RELEASE CADENCE

Bi-weekly releases for bug fixes and minor updates. Quarterly releases for new components and large-scale changes, with a roadmap defined each quarter alongside design stakeholders.

COMMUNICATION CHANNELS

A quarterly newsletter with each release. A dedicated Teams channel for design systems questions. Quarterly office hours open to the broader team.

IMPACT

1,000+ implementations in 3 weeks. 105,000+ in 10 months.

Adoption that grew steadily... and kept growing.

Just three weeks post-launch, the Data Token alone had been used over 1,000 times by designers from 6 teams. A year later, it had accumulated over 105,000 inserts across the org, making it the most actively used DNA component by a significant margin. The kit itself has grown steadily in usage, now adopted by 71 teams.

The strategy rollout in Q4 2025 extended that reach. The DNA Design Systems SharePoint portal received 379 total views from 76 unique designers in its first few months, with viewership up 19% in the most recent 30-day period, indicating sustained and growing engagement.
